1. Overview of the Proposed Solution
1.1 Problem and core idea
Assignment 2 showed that rural teachers in Sabah face a
specific and well-documented gap in professional
development. Teachers at disconnected schools cannot
reliably access online CPD, there is no portable way to
record classroom application, and district and national
offices are losing their main evidence source as SPLKPM
shuts down in 2026 (Utusan Malaysia, 2025). These
problems are not new: weak infrastructure, heavy
workloads, and professional isolation have been
consistently reported among rural Sabah teachers (Jaafar
et al., 2025), and existing platforms like DELIMa 2.0
were built for connectivity conditions that most Interior
schools simply lack.
CikguSync is our proposed response to that gap. The
name draws directly from the context: 'cikgu' is the
everyday Malay word for teacher, and 'sync' describes
what the platform does technically, storing CPD progress
and classroom evidence on the device first and uploading
it whenever a connection becomes available. CikguSync
is built as a progressive web app that teachers can install
or pin on anto theiroid phonephones basic workflow is:
download a small CPD module bundle during a morning
4G window or at a NADI or PKG centre, complete the
module offline at any point, capture a low-resolution
photo as classroom evidence, tag it to an SGM 2.0
domain, and let the app sync the record later when
connectivity returns.
The logo on the title page reflects the same ideas. An
open book sits at the centre as a symbol of professional
learning. Circular arrows around it represent the
offline-first sync cycle. The diamond pattern along the
book's spine is inspired by linangkit, a traditional
embroidery style from Sabah's Lotud and Rungus
communities, which grounds the design in Sabah
specifically rather than in generic edtech imagery. The
colour palette follows the navy used in Malaysian
Ministry of Education materials and stays legible as a
phone home-screen icon.
CikguSync is not meant to replace DELIMa 2.0, PPGB
2.0, Program Duta Guru, UNICEF FS4A, or MDEC's
#CikguJuaraDigital. Those programmes are already
embedded in the system and represent substantial
investment. CikguSync sits alongside them as a
lightweight, offline-tolerant layer that can eventually
exchange structured records with these systems once
institutional agreements are in place.
1.2 Target users and value proposition
Three personas from Assignment 2 anchor the design.
Cikgu Liana, a primary teacher in Nabawan, uses offline
modules to learn practical techniques, photographs
classroom evidence, and syncs her record when a
connection is available. Guru Besar Jainal, a school
leader in Pitas, receives a weekly school summary by
Telegram or email rather than being expected to manage
another platform. Pegawai Norhaida, a PPD district
officer in Keningau, can access an aggregated dashboard
that includes data from offline schools and exports
records for SSH and DPD reporting.
2. Preconditions and Interoperability
2.1 Preconditions for use
CikguSync is designed for low-resource conditions, but it
still rests on a few basic assumptions. Teachers need
access to a personal or school Android phone capable of
running a modern browser. Continuous internet is not
required, but occasional connectivity is needed to
download modules and sync records. This can happen
during a morning 4G window, at a NADI or PKG centre,
at home on weekends, or during a district visit. On the
institutional side, BPG and PPD would need to approve
the CPD module structure and its mapping to SGM 2.0
domains. School leaders need a simple accepted channel
for receiving summaries, such as Telegram or email, and
district officers need at least one stable online location for
reviewing synced data and exporting reports.
The solution deliberately avoids requirements that would
be unrealistic in most Interior schools. It does not depend
on high-speed internet, continuous cloud access, devices
for every pupil, or a school-side administrator. The
prototype also does not require native app store
deployment. Leaving these out was a deliberate choice
based on what the context realistically allows.
2.2 Social interoperability
Social interoperability means the solution fits the
governance structures and everyday routines already in
place around it. CikguSync positions BPG as the owner
of professional development and uses SGM 2.0 as the
recognition language for teacher growth (MOE, 2023), so
it does not introduce a new competency framework that
teachers and administrators would need to learn
separately. It translates what teachers already do into
evidence that counts within an existing national standard.
At school level, the Guru Besar receives useful
summaries without taking on a technical administrator
role. At the district level, PPD and JPN officers use an
aggregated dashboard to see which schools are
progressing, which remain effectively offline, and where
support visits or NADI/PKG sessions should be directed
next.
2.3 Technical interoperability
The prototype is standalone but built to be
integration-ready. Data is stored using SGM 2.0 domain
codes together with teacher and school identifiers, so
records can later be exported or connected to DELIMa
2.0, Program Duta Guru, UNICEF FS4A, or BPG/JPN
reporting systems without restructuring the data model.
The system exposes simple REST endpoints and supports
CSV and JSON export. Full integration is not promised at
the prototype stage because it would require formal
agreements with MOE units and partner organisations,
which are outside the scope of a student project.

3.1 Main concepts and class diagram
The system has four main concept groups: users and
organisations, learning objects, evidence and sync, and
reporting. The UML class diagram in Figure 1 shows the
static data structure. In the model, a Teacher completes
CPDModule items through ModuleProgress, creates
EvidenceSubmission items, and maps each submission to
an SGMDomain. This lets the system connect teacher
activity to SGM 2.0.
When the device is offline, EvidenceSubmission items
are held in the SyncQueue. A weekly summary then
aggregates evidence at the school level for the school
leader to view.
Three design decisions in the model are important.
First, EvidenceSubmission and SyncQueue are kept as
separate entities. This is better than using one record with
a status field because low-connectivity uploads may be
retried several times. If the same evidence is uploaded
twice, the API can use evidenceId to accept only one
submission while still keeping retry information in the
sync queue for troubleshooting.
Second, ModuleProgress is separated from
EvidenceSubmission because completing a module and
capturing classroom evidence may happen at different
times. A teacher might finish a module today and only
collect classroom evidence two weeks later. These actions
need different timestamps and different meanings in the
audit trail.
Third, SGMDomain is an explicit class with a domainId
and an indicatorCode, rather than as a hard-coded string
field. If the SGM 2.0 framework changes in the future, or
if an additional UNESCO ICT-CFT mapping is added
(UNESCO, 2018), the framework can be updated as data
rather than through a major schema change.
3.2 User interaction flow
The main user flow in Figure 2 begins with the teacher,
since Option 1 focuses on the rural teacher CPD loop.
The teacher opens the PWA, downloads a module during
a connectivity window, completes it offline, captures
evidence, maps it to an SGM 2.0 domain, and syncs the
submission later.
The flow is linear. Each step represents a teacher action
that must work without an internet connection, while
synchronisation is delayed until connectivity becomes
available. This matches teachers’ experience of the
system: a sequence of ordinary teaching and CPD
activities, with the network used only when available.
The flow is also suitable for demonstration. The network
can be turned off while the teacher completes the module
and captures evidence, then turned back on to show how
the queued submission syncs automatically.
3.3 System architecture
The architecture, as shown in Figure 3, has a teacher
PWA, an intermittent connectivity layer, a Supabase
prototype backend, and two lightweight reading views.
The teacher PWA contains an offline cache, local
IndexedDB storage, evidence capture, and a sync worker.
The backend contains authentication, a relational
database, object storage for compressed evidence images,
REST API endpoints, and row-level security rules.
School leaders and district officers read summaries from
the same backend rather than maintaining separate
spreadsheets, so reporting reflects a single source of truth.
3.4 Offline submission and failure handling
Offline operation is the main requirement, not an extra
feature. Figure 4 shows the control flow. When a teacher
submits evidence offline, the app stores the image and
metadata locally, adds the item to the sync queue, checks
connectivity from time to time, and uploads only when a
connection is available. Sync must be idempotent, so
repeated uploads of the same item do not create duplicate
records in the dashboard.
The design handles three common failure cases.
First, if the network drops during upload, the item returns
to the queue. The retry counter increases and the app
waits longer before trying again.
Second, if the item still fails after a set limit, for example
ten attempts over 14 days, it is marked as failed. The
teacher then sees a clear "tap to retry" option, so the
record is not silently lost.
Third, pupil privacy is protected through face blurring.
The first version uses on-device face detection to blur
faces before the image enters the queue. A server-side
check runs after sync. This process should not block the
teacher unless the image is unusable. The goal is to
reduce risk while keeping the workflow practical in
classrooms with low light, crowded rooms, or older
phones.
Multi-device conflict handling is out of scope for the first
release because the design assumes one teacher uses one
main device.
4. Technology Infrastructure and Design
Choices
4.1 Mobile vs web-based
CikguSync is built as a progressive web app, a website
that behaves like an app. Teachers can open it in a
browser, save it to their Android home screen, use it
offline, and get updates automatically without ever
visiting an app store. That matters because downloading
and updating a traditional app requires things many rural
teachers don't always have: a stable connection, a Google
account, and a device that can handle the process
smoothly.
4.2 Online vs offline functionality
The system is built to work offline first. Teachers can
open modules they've already downloaded, complete

activities, write reflections, capture evidence, and save
their submissions, all without needing a connection.
Android Chrome can quietly delete stored data when the
phone runs low on space or when a user clears their
browser, so local storage isn't treated as permanent. Think
of it more like a working copy that can be topped up the
next time a teacher reaches an NADI or PKG centre. A
connection is only needed for three things: downloading
new modules, syncing saved records, and refreshing the
dashboard. This was a deliberate decision shaped by the
reality on the ground in rural Sabah. When the school
router cuts out, rain breaks the 4G signal, or a teacher
only gets online for a few minutes a day, the system still
needs to do its job. With somewhere between 300 and
400 schools in Sabah still without internet access (The
Star, 2025), building around connectivity would mean
failing the schools that need this the most.
4.3 Cloud vs local hosting
Supabase is a realistic backend for the student prototype
because it gives you authentication, Postgres tables,
object storage, REST APIs, and row-level security, and it
does so quickly. For a real-world public sector
deployment, we propose to migrate the backend to
MOE/BPG-controlled hosting or an approved Malaysian
government cloud so that long-term ownership and
control of teacher data is retained by the Malaysian
education system. Supabase is useful for demonstration
purposes; production data residency and ownership
decisions would be made by BPG in accordance with
MOE policy.
4.4 Integration, voice, and text
The first release is standalone but integration-ready. Full
integration with DELIMa 2.0 or partner systems is
postponed because it requires institutional access and API
agreements. The data model, however, already uses SGM
2.0 domains and exportable records. The core interface is
text-first because text is easier to translate, compress,
cache, and search. Optional short audio summaries can
support lower-literacy users or parent-facing
explanations, but audio is not the main interaction mode
because it increases data use, and Sabah’s linguistic
diversity makes reliable speech-to-text impractical
without a custom acoustic model.
5. Human Aspects
5.1 Digital literacy and familiar behaviour
The solution assumes teachers are comfortable with basic
phone actions: opening WhatsApp, taking a photo,
downloading a file, and checking a status icon. It does not
assume any confidence with cloud tools or education
platforms beyond that.
The teacher interface uses plain labels such as "Download
module", "Use offline", "Add evidence", "Saved on this
phone", and "Sync when online". The school-leader
summary is designed to feel like the WhatsApp reporting
routines most school leaders already use. Both choices
reduce the number of new behaviours required and lower
the barrier to first use.
5.2 Language accessibility
The launch interface is available in Bahasa Malaysia and
English. Bahasa Malaysia is the default because it is the
national language of instruction. English covers CPD
content and technical terms where Bahasa Malaysia
equivalents are less established.
Kadazandusun and Iban are supported as addable
language packs for selected module summaries rather
than full-system translations in the first release. Sabah's
linguistic diversity is real, but translating the entire
platform into all local languages is not achievable in a
prototype. The first batch of Kadazandusun summaries
would be co-authored with #CikguJuaraDigital Champion
Teachers from the Interior Division (Sabah Media, 2024),
so the content reflects how teachers in that area actually
speak rather than a generic translation.
5.3 Accessibility, inclusion, and workload
Rural Sabah primary teachers are predominantly women,
and the design takes this into account. A teacher with
childcare or family responsibilities at home often cannot
stay at school late to use an evening 4G window. The
morning-download model fits her daily routine better
than a sync that requires her to be at school after hours.
Data costs are also a practical constraint: a 40 GB
Celcom SIM in Sabah costs around RM35, and a single
live webinar can exhaust the entire allowance in one
session. Keeping module bundles under 25 MB and
relying on image-based evidence means monthly CPD
data use stays well below 200 MB, so professional
development does not compete with family
communication or pupils' homework support.
For older teachers or those less confident with digital
tools, the same plain labels that help new users also help
here: "Download module", "Use offline", "Add
evidence", "Saved on this phone", "Sync when online".
These are paired with high contrast, large tap targets, and
clear sync status indicators. The interface meets WCAG
2.1 AA contrast and tap-target standards. Optional audio
summaries support teachers with low vision and parents
with limited literacy, and no essential information is
conveyed through colour alone.
Pupils are indirect beneficiaries rather than direct users.
The data rules in Section 6 protect them: faces are blurred
before upload, names are discouraged in reflections, and
the first release has no pupil-facing component.
Vulnerable pupils, including orphans, children of
refugees from across the Indonesian border, and children
with disabilities, are not made more visible to the
platform than any of their peers.
Rural teachers are also at risk of being marginalised by
measurement systems built around online activity, which
tend to count connected schools far more readily than
disconnected ones. CikguSync records offline activity
and syncs it later, so a teacher working at a school
without internet is not logged as inactive simply because
of where she was posted.

6.1 Data collected
CikguSync collects only data needed for CPD access,
evidence logging, syncing, and reporting. It does not
collect pupil names, precise GPS coordinates, private
family data, or unrelated teacher performance data. The
main data groups are:
• Teacher profile: name, school, role, and language
for account and CPD record purposes.
• Module progress: started/completed status and
downloaded modules for learning continuity.
• Evidence metadata: SGM domain, module, date,
and short reflection for CPD recognition.
• Evidence image: low resolution classroom photo
with faces blurred before upload.
• Sync metadata: queued time, synced time, retry
count, and audit status for reliability.
• Aggregated dashboard data: school and district
counts for support planning and reporting.
6.2 Storage, access, and control
To support offline use, the records are temporarily stored
on the teacher’s device using browser storage (e.g.,
IndexedDB). When the internet is available, the sync
worker uploads the records to the backend. The evidence
images are stored separately from the metadata and are
compressed. The device blurs faces before upload so no
identifiable images of pupils should leave the school.
Access is role-based. Teachers can see their own
modules, evidence and CPD record. Guru Besar see
school level summaries and limited teacher level progress
if needed for support. PPD officers can see district data
and summaries of schools. BPG and JPN get compiled
reports and exports. Only once sharing has been
approved, external partners can see records relevant to
their programme.
Data ownership should remain with MOE/BPG and the
Malaysian public education system. Technical providers
should act as processors under public-sector rules and
contract limits, in line with the Personal Data Protection
(Amendment) Act 2024.
6.3 Misuse prevention, transparency, and
accountability
• Data minimisation: collect only what is needed for
CPD recognition and support.
• On-device anonymisation: blur pupil faces before
upload and discourage pupil names in reflections.
• Role-based access: apply row-level security so users
see only appropriate records.
• Audit logs: record when data is created, changed,
viewed, exported, or deleted.
• Aggregated reporting: use school and district
summaries where individual data is not required.
• Retention rules: keep evidence for a defined CPD
cycle, then archive or delete according to MOE
policy.
Transparency. A context-aware transparency feature is
built into the teacher interface. Each evidence item shows
a simple "Who can see this?" label, for example Only me,
My school leader, PPD officer, or Aggregated for BPG
reporting. After syncing, the teacher receives a data-use
receipt showing what was uploaded, when it was synced,
which SGM domain it supports, and who can access it.
Accountability. Three accountability mechanisms answer
the question "who is responsible when something goes
wrong?" First, audit logs are reviewed quarterly by a
designated PPD data officer, with anomalies (mass
exports, unusual access patterns) flagged automatically.
Second, an anonymised summary of what data was
collected and which partners read it is published quarterly
on the JPN Sabah website and circulated through PIBG
WhatsApp groups, modelled on UNICEF FS4A reporting
practices (UNICEF Malaysia, 2024). third, each PPD
designates a serving teacher, not an administrator, as a
data ombudsperson. The ombudsperson receives
complaints from peers and escalates them through JPN
Sabah. This recognises the asymmetry between an
individual rural teacher and a national platform, and
adapts a governance pattern already familiar from
community-level Native Court roles.
7. Updated Requirements
7.1 Requirements retained and changed
After moving deeper into the solution domain, the core
requirements remain valid: downloadable CPD
micro-modules, offline completion, image-based
evidence, queue-and-sync, SGM 2.0 mapping, a portable
CPD record, low data use, Android compatibility, privacy
protection, and basic school and district views. The main
change is scope reduction. Assignment 3 selects Option 1
as the first release and keeps mentoring only as a later or
lightweight extension.
• Teacher CPD: keep as the main product loop and
prototype focus.
• Evidence: keep image-based classroom evidence,
with on-device face blurring and low-resolution
upload.
• Sync: keep queue-and-sync as the main demo
scenario.
• Mentoring (the focus of ContextSquaRE 2 in
Assignment 2): deferred. The data model supports it
through later extension of evidence submissions, but
the prototype concentrates on the teacher CPD loop
from ContextSquaRE 1.
• School-leader view: keep minimal weekly summary,
with no daily login expectation.
• District dashboard: keep simple SGM 2.0
dashboard and export; postpone full partner
integration.
• Languages: launch Bahasa Malaysia and English;
add Kadazandusun summaries for selected modules
later.
• Integration: prototype is standalone but API and
export-ready; full integrations with DELIMa, Duta
Guru, and FS4A are postponed.

8. Conclusion
CikguSync is a small ICT solution for teacher
professional development in rural Sabah. The technical
design is based on the local conditions of connectivity,
governance structures of schools, language diversity and
existing Malaysian education programs, rather than
assuming technology alone solves the problem. The
offline-first CPD loop focus means the solution works
directly for Cikgu Liana but still gives Jainal dan
Norhaida the summaries they need. The result is realistic
to prototype, treats sensitive school data responsibly, and
fits well enough in the wider education ecosystem to be
extended after the first release.
