1. Use Case, Primary Stakeholder,
and Strategic Goal
1.1 Use case and geographic focus
The use case is digital and hybrid teacher training
in rural Malaysia under low-resource conditions.
We focus on the Interior Division of Sabah
(Keningau, Tambunan, Tenom, Nabawan, and
Sipitang) and nearby rural districts (Pitas, Tongod,
and Ranau). Three facts make this a good case: eight
of the ten poorest districts in Malaysia are in Sabah;
the Sabah State Minister of Education said in
December 2025 that 300 to 400 rural Sabah schools
still have no internet (The Star, 2025) and a 2025
UMS study found that rural Sabah teachers face weak
infrastructure, heavy workloads, and professional
isolation (Jaafar et al., 2025).
1.2 Primary stakeholder
We assume the primary stakeholder is the Bahagian
Profesionalisme Guru (BPG), the unit inside the
Ministry of Education Malaysia (MOE) in charge of
teacher professional development. It is led by its
Director, Datuk Dr. Mistirine binti Radin. BPG sets
training policy, plans CPD programmes, checks that
in-service training meets national standards, and
evaluates programmes yearly (MOE, n.d.-a). It sits in
the same sector as the Institut Pendidikan Guru
Malaysia (IPGM) and the Institut Aminuddin Baki
(IAB) (E-Bantuan Malaysia, 2025). BPG is the right
stakeholder here because it owns teacher training and
the main programmes behind it, such as PPGB 2.0
and SGM 2.0 (MOE, 2023; Slideshare, 2024).
1.3 Strategic goal
From BPG's point of view, the strategic goal is to
improve training for rural teachers in Malaysia,
with a focus on Sabah's Interior Division, so
teachers can grow their digital teaching skills in
real rural conditions. The goal aligns with three
national plans: the Dasar Pendidikan Digital
(DPD) 2023–2030, the Thirteenth Malaysia Plan
(RMK-13, 2026–2030), and the Standard Guru
Malaysia 2.0.
1.4 Where the goal comes from
The goal reflects both national policy and the real
rural problems from the source material: weak
internet, not enough devices, and poor follow-up after
training. We assume four pressures on BPG are
driving it.
First, national policy. The DPD 2023–2030,
launched by Minister Fadhlina Sidek on 28
November 2023, commits MOE to producing
"digitally fluent pupils." It reports that only 2.2% of
Malaysian teachers are at advanced digital level
and 39.9% at intermediate, with rural areas worse
off (MOE, 2023). RMK-13, tabled on 31 July 2025,
keeps a "Raise the Floor" pillar for underserved
regions. Together, these plans make rural teacher
training a measurable target.
Second, a teacher shortage. On 9 April 2025, MOE
launched the Pelan Tindakan Nasional Pendidikan
2025–2030 to handle a shortage of almost 10,000
teachers in Maths, English, and science, mostly in
Sabah, Sarawak, Kelantan, and Terengganu. MOE
plans to recruit 12,000 new teachers from July 2025,
plus a six-month intensive programme (PLIG) for
new graduates (PrezCom, 2025). Many will be posted
to rural schools, making PPGB 2.0 and its follow-up
support very important.
Third, a change in how training is tracked. On 30
December 2025, Minister Fadhlina Sidek announced
that the central training tracking system (SPLKPM)
will be shut down in 2026, to "place full trust" in
teachers (Utusan Malaysia, 2025). Removing this
system just as DPD 2023–2030 and RMK-13 start
means any new solution needs its own way to track
training.
Fourth, state-level plans match. JPN Sabah, under
Director Raisin bin Saidin, runs the Sepakat Sabah
Hebat (SSH) 2023–2025 plan. Its four pillars mirror
the national Program Transformasi Sekolah 2025
(TS25): Effective Leaders, Quality Teachers, PTA
and Community Support, and Meaningful Learning
Environment (JPN Sabah, 2022). The "Quality
Teachers" pillar matches any BPG plan for rural
digital training, making Sabah a natural fit.

The strategic goal involves groups that run the work,
groups that influence it, and groups affected by the
result. We list them below with specific names.

Mistirine binti Radin owns the teacher training policy
and the SGM 2.0 standard. Bahagian Sumber dan

3. Project Goals and
ContextSquaREs
From the strategic goal in Section 1, we identify three
project goals, one from each of the three stakeholder
views: the teacher, the school leader, and the people
who track results at the district and national levels.
Project Goal 1 (teacher view). Rural teachers should
be able to get and use practical training even when
the internet and devices are limited.
Project Goal 2 (school leader view). Schools should
support teacher learning through simple peer
learning, mentoring, and coaching.
Project Goal 3 (tracking view). District and national
offices should be able to track, recognise, and scale
up rural teacher training based on real evidence.
3.1 ContextSquaRE 1 — Project Goal 1
Project goal. Rural teachers should be able to get and
use practical training even when the internet and
devices are limited.
Stakeholders. Rural teachers in Sabah Interior, Guru
Besar, rural pupils, district coaches from JPN Sabah
and the PPDs, BPG as national policy owner, BSTP
as owner of DELIMa 2.0, and MCMC NADI centres
as community infrastructure.
1. Key Challenges. Teachers in the Interior Division
deal with weak 4G, few devices, and pupils with
different resources at home. As of December 2025,
300 to 400 rural Sabah schools still have no internet.
Time is tight: a new teacher at a Rural 2 school has
extra paperwork, drives three or more hours to the
nearest PKG teacher centre, and has little time for
live online training. Professional isolation is a known
problem (Jaafar et al., 2025). Teachers usually stay
only two to three years before asking for a transfer,
which makes long training investments less useful.
2. Existing Solutions. Malaysia already has useful
pieces in place. DELIMa 2.0 includes a professional
development module, short certified micro-courses, a
teacher knowledge-sharing space, and single sign-on
to Apple, Microsoft, and Google learning tools
(BSTP, 2025). PPGB 2.0 gives new teachers a
structured induction and mentoring programme
(Slideshare, 2024). MDEC's #CikguJuaraDigital has
30 Champion Teachers in Sabah who have trained
1,434 others since 2020 (Sabah Media, 2024). The
131 MCMC NADI centres have internet and training
space, and the NADI Ambassador Sabah pilot (24
July 2025) reached teachers for the first time (Borneo
Post, 2025a). These structures provide a policy and
platform base rather than a blank starting point.
3. Shortcomings. Existing support is scattered and
not well adapted to rural conditions. Getting training
does not always mean teachers apply it in the
classroom, especially when the training is generic or
needs a stable connection. DELIMa 2.0 assumes the

user is always online; a 2022 study in Sibu already
showed similar teacher-adoption and connectivity
problems (Keling et al., 2025). DELIMa has no
Kadazandusun or Iban content for teachers. BPG has
not published a mapping from SGM 2.0 to UNESCO
ICT-CFT (UNESCO, 2018), which makes rigorous
curriculum design harder. PKGs and IAB are too far
to reach in a normal workday, and NADI centres are
not officially linked to BPG's in-service training
system.
4. Proposed Solution Direction. A digital solution
should provide mobile-friendly, low-bandwidth,
offline-first training content, ready-to-use classroom
templates, and asynchronous evidence submission,
written against SGM 2.0 and DPD 2023–2030 skill
levels. It should work on cheap Android devices and
sync whenever a connection is available — over 4G,
NADI Wi-Fi, or USB sync during a PPD visit.
Content should be built with #CikguJuaraDigital
Champion Teachers and include at least Bahasa
Malaysia and Kadazandusun for relevant modules.
Training done should count toward PPB credits under
SGM 2.0, so teachers keep their progress when they
move schools.
3.2 ContextSquaRE 2 — Project Goal 2
Project goal. Schools should support teacher learning
through simple peer learning, mentoring, and
coaching.
Stakeholders. Guru Besar and Penolong Kanan at
rural schools; IAB Cawangan Sabah for school leader
training; BPG as national policy owner; JPN Sabah
and the PPDs; mentor teachers including
#CikguJuaraDigital; the PKG teacher centres in each
Interior district; and pupils who benefit from better
teaching.
1. Key Challenges. Interior schools are small; a
typical rural primary school has 4 to 12 teachers ,
which makes it hard to run a full peer learning group
inside one school. Guru Besar are often new leaders
themselves, with little protected time for mentoring.
Schools in the same cluster can be three or more
hours apart on unpaved roads, and high turnover
breaks up any peer learning that starts. When peer
support happens, it is usually on WhatsApp or
Telegram: not saved, not official, not recognised.
Rural timetables have no dedicated time for peer
learning.
2. Existing Solutions. IAB Cawangan Sabah runs
structured training for school leaders (including the
NPQEL pathway) for Sabah and WP Labuan (IAB,
2025). The PKG network has 367 centres nationwide
and is the only MOE training site physically present
in every Interior district (PKG KPM, 2025). SSH
2023–2025 lists “Quality Teachers” as one of four
pillars, giving JPN Sabah a mandate for peer learning
work (JPN Sabah, 2022). SGM 2.0's "Community
Engagement" domain recognises peer learning as a
teacher skill (MOE, 2023). PPGB 2.0 gives new
teachers a structured induction and mentoring phase.
3. Shortcomings. IAB's training happens in Kota
Kinabalu or Tuaran — six to ten hours from some
Interior districts — so small schools cannot send their
leader to multi-day courses. PKG visits take teachers
away from already short-staffed classrooms.
WhatsApp groups carry no recognition, no archive,
no quality control, and vanish when a teacher
transfers. There is no cross-school mentoring system
after PPGB 2.0 induction ends. SSH 2023–2025
targets are set at state level, not at the cluster or
school level where peer learning actually happens.
4. Proposed Solution Direction. A simple digital
coordination layer — not a new platform — should
let mentor teachers and #CikguJuaraDigital schedule
short asynchronous check-ins with mentees across
schools, share lesson materials in a searchable cluster
folder, and log activity against SGM 2.0 "Community
Engagement" indicators. It should work with the PKG
cluster structure, not replace it. Guru Besar should see
it as a dashboard without a new admin login.
Recognition should attach to small concrete actions
(one lesson shared, one mentoring check-in, one peer
observation) instead of bulk attendance hours.
Offline-first is required: materials made on a day with
no internet must be submittable later from a NADI
centre or the teacher's home town on weekend trips.
3.3 ContextSquaRE 3 — Project Goal 3
Project goal. District and national offices should be
able to track, recognise, and scale up rural teacher
training based on real evidence.
Stakeholders. BPG as national owner of training
quality and the SGM 2.0 standard; JPN Sabah and the
PPDs; BSTP as owner of DELIMa 2.0; IAB
Cawangan Sabah for school leader results; MCMC
for connectivity data; and Yayasan PETRONAS and
UNICEF Malaysia as outside partners with their own
tracking (Program Duta Guru and FS4A).
1. Key Challenges. On 30 December 2025, Minister
Fadhlina Sidek announced that SPLKPM, BPG's
central training tracking system, will be shut down in
2026 (Utusan Malaysia, 2025). This happens just as
DPD 2023–2030 and RMK-13 start asking for more
measured results. No public dataset breaks down
teacher digital skill levels for Sabah Interior schools,
and the 300 to 400 unconnected schools leave no
trace in platform analytics (The Star, 2025). The data
we do have is about attendance, not about what
changed in the classroom. Rural teachers, being
offline more often, leave less digital trace, so
measurement is biased toward the already-connected.
There is no shared dashboard between JPN Sabah and
BPG for rural teachers as a group.
2. Existing Solutions. SGM 2.0 includes a
self-assessment tool covering four skill domains
(MOE, 2023). DELIMa 2.0 has some teacher-side
Page 4

analytics for modules finished and micro-credentials
earned (BSTP, 2025). SSH 2023–2025 has a
four-pillar framework linked to TS25 (JPN Sabah,
2022). BPG promises yearly impact studies covering
100% of its programmes (MOE, n.d.-a). Outside
partners track their own work: Program Duta Guru
tracks progress toward 4,500 STEM teachers by 2030
(Yayasan PETRONAS, 2024); FS4A reports on reach
and use in both online and offline teacher modes
(UNICEF Malaysia, 2024).
3. Shortcomings. Attendance and hours are poor
proxies for teaching change, which is one reason
SPLKPM was shut down. SGM 2.0 self-assessment is
not checked or combined across schools. DELIMa
2.0 analytics only cover online schools, excluding the
ones that need help most. SSH targets sit at state level
and do not break down to cluster or teacher. BPG's
impact studies come out too late to change in-year
spending. Outside-partner tracking is not connected
to BPG's, so a teacher doing both Program Duta Guru
and #CikguJuaraDigital is counted in their systems
but not nationally. No tracking method exists for the
rural teacher group that DPD 2023–2030 will be
judged on.
4. Proposed Solution Direction. A light,
offline-tolerant logging tool should support, not
replace, the SGM 2.0 self-assessment. It should log
teacher activity even at disconnected schools and
sync when a connection is available. Recognition
should link to SGM 2.0 skill domains (knowledge,
instruction, community, and personal quality) rather
than raw hours, so teachers do not carry the
compliance burden that SPLKPM's shutdown was
meant to remove. A district dashboard should serve
JPN Sabah and the PPDs, while BPG gets a combined
national view for DPD 2023–2030 and RMK-13
reporting. BPG should publish a mapping from SGM
2.0 to UNESCO ICT-CFT so the dashboard uses
shared skill categories. The tool should be compatible
with Program Duta Guru and FS4A from the start, so
teacher work is counted once and visible everywhere.

flagged the internet issue to the State Education

Problems
Liana wishes to receive more training to be able to
provide better classes for the students, however the
closest training location is in Kota Kinabalu, which is

Needs
Short lessons that do not require live presence, but are
downloadable at a small memory and low bandwidth,
so she can use the morning 4G at the school to do so.
The lessons should be working offline as well, and
easily transferable to paper if needed. The content
should be in Bahasa Malaysia and include summaries
for teachers in Kadazandusun to hand out to the
student’s parents. There should also be a way for her
to keep track of her CPD record when she transfers.
Existing solutions
There are various small crumbs available, like
watching YouTube videos when she is in Keningau
town, or copy lesson ideas from Telegram/WhatsApp
groups. There was a way to track CPD records, but
unfortunately SPLKPM will be shut down in 2026
(Utasan Malaysia, 2025), so right now keeping track
on paper is a solution.

5. Assumptions for Requirement
Analysis
Assumption 1: teachers in Sabah Interior have their
own phones (Android), however they cannot use 4G
throughout the day, or at a high bandwidth. Often the
internet connection only works in the mornings.
Assumption 2: the Bahagian Profesionalisme Guru
(BPG) will use the SGM 2.0 because the SPLKPM
will not continue in 2026 so there is no other way to
log CPD records (Utusan Malaysia, 2025; Ministry of
Education Malaysia, 2023).
Assumption 3: we assume that the solution will not
replace DELIMa, PPGB, #CikguJuaraDigital,
Program Duta Guru, and UNICEF FS4A, but it
should integrate with them. There are already
substantial investments made in these programs,
which we do not want to become unused.
Assumption 4: we also assumed that on average a
teacher stays about 2-3 years at a Sabah Interior
school before transferring. We could not find any
explicit district-level tenure statistics published
online.
Assumption 5: there are more than thirty languages in
Sabah, so we used a default language Bahasa
Malaysia. Kadazundusun, Iban and English were
added for some of the modules.
6. Requirements for our Digital
Solution
6.1 Functional requirements
Functional requirement 1: the solution needs to have
an option with small modules for CPD that can be
used on smartphones. They should be able to be
downloaded so it can later be watched without access
to the internet.
Functional requirement 2: the solution focuses more
on processing images rather than videos because of
the lower bandwidth internet availability. It should be
easy for Liana to upload images that account for her
classroom evidence, e.g., for SGM 2.0 skill domains.
Functional requirement 3: the solution has a smart
option where you can queue your submissions and
keep them loaded on the app. Until there is a steady
internet connection, the submissions will be uploaded
online.
Functional requirement 4: the solution should have a
mentoring dashboard where mentors and mentees can
see their check-ins and search for lessons in their own
folders. This will help Jainal.
Functional requirement 5: the solution is able to
provide the option to log shared lessons, peer
observations, mentoring check-ins, and the submitted
evidence. This is used for the SGM 2.0 PPB.
Functional requirement 6: for Guru Besar it is
important to receive a summary view for the entire
school without having to login, for example it can be
sent to his WhatsApp/Telegram on a weekly basis.
Functional requirement 7: the solution also provides a
dashboard for the district for PDD/JPN Sabah for
SGM 2.0. The self-assessments, recognition events,
and other program participation can be shown here.
This is important for Norhaida.
Functional requirement 8: the solution should be
available in different languages, depending on the
availability of all 30 languages, we can add a variety
of languages. The user just chooses their language
once (because switching languages will use internet
connection).
Functional requirement 9: the solution solves the
issue for the CPD log recordings. It will be possible
to log everything using the solution.
6.2 Non-functional requirements
NFR1 (Connectivity): the mobile client must work
fully offline for at least 14 days between syncs. A
complete CPD micro-module bundle must not exceed
25 MB so that teachers like Liana can download it
during the morning window when 4G is available at
school.
NFR2 (Device compatibility): the mobile client must
run on Android 11 or higher with no more than 2 GB
RAM and a maximum installed size of 200 MB. This
reflects that most rural Sabah teachers own mid-range
Android phones rather than high-end devices.
NFR3 (Performance): any screen in the app must load
within 3 seconds on a Redmi Note 12-class device,
regardless of whether the device is online or offline at
that moment.
NFR4 (Data cost): a typical month of use, covering
four modules and a set of evidence submissions, must
consume no more than 200 MB of mobile data. This
keeps the solution within reach of teachers on a 40
GB monthly SIM card who cannot afford to spend all
their data on one platform.
NFR5 (Localisation): the interface must be available
in Bahasa Malaysia and English from launch.
Kadazandusun and Iban strings must be addable
without changing the underlying code, so that new
Page 7

-- 7 of 10 --

language support can be rolled out as content
becomes available.
NFR6 (Accessibility): the interface must meet
WCAG 2.1 AA standards for colour contrast and tap
target size. Parent-facing audio summaries must
include narration to support lower-literacy users.
NFR7 (Security and privacy): all teacher and pupil
data must be stored in line with Malaysia's Personal
Data Protection Act 2010, as amended in 2024. Pupil
faces in evidence photos must be blurred
automatically on the device before upload, so that no
identifiable images leave the school.
NFR8 (Interoperability): the system must expose
REST APIs aligned to SGM 2.0 and UNESCO
ICT-CFT v3 so that DELIMa 2.0, Program Duta
Guru, and UNICEF FS4A can pull or push data
without manual reconciliation by Norhaida or her
colleagues.
NFR9 (Reliability): syncing must be idempotent,
meaning that re-uploading a queued evidence item
never creates a duplicate entry in the district
dashboard. This matters most for teachers who submit
from NADI centres on weekend trips after
accumulating a backlog.
NFR10 (Maintainability): no school-side
administrator role should be needed. All admin
functions must sit at PPD level or above, so that Guru
Besar like Jainal are not burdened with platform
management on top of their existing workload.
NFR11 (Sustainability): hosting must use federal
cloud procurement such as MyGovCloud or an
equivalent, so that the per-school operating cost is
zero and the solution does not collapse when project
funding ends.
NFR12 (Auditability): all SGM 2.0 recognition
events must be timestamped, attributable to a specific
teacher, and exportable in a format that replaces the
audit function previously handled by SPLKPM.
6.3 MoSCoW prioritisation
Must have: FR1, FR2, FR3, FR5, FR9, NFR1, NFR2,
NFR3, NFR4, NFR7, NFR9, and NFR12. Together
these form the minimum viable loop: a teacher at a
disconnected school can access training content,
submit evidence offline, and have it recognised
against SGM 2.0 once a connection is available.
Without this core, Liana cannot benefit from the
solution at all, and the audit gap left by the shutdown
of SPLKPM in 2026 remains open.
Should have: FR4, FR6, FR7, FR8 limited to
DELIMa 2.0 and Program Duta Guru, NFR5 for
Kadazandusun in selected modules, NFR6, NFR8,
NFR10, and NFR11. These requirements make the
solution useful for Jainal and Norhaida and connect it
to the existing programmes described in Assumption
3. They are not strictly needed for an individual
teacher to complete a module, but without them the
school leader and district officer perspectives from
ContextSquaREs 2 and 3 receive little value and the
solution risks running as a parallel structure next to
DELIMa 2.0 rather than integrating with it.
Could have: FR8 extended to UNICEF FS4A, and
NFR5 extended to Iban. These add real value but
depend on conditions that are not guaranteed for a
first release: partner agreements with UNICEF
Malaysia, and enough Iban-speaking Champion
Teachers available through the #CikguJuaraDigital
network to co-author content.
Won't have in this release: a pupil-facing client,
parent-facing content beyond audio summaries,
integration with KDCA cultural-content authoring
tools, and any feature that requires a continuous
school-side internet connection. These are excluded
to keep the first release focused on the conditions
Liana actually faces, as documented by the Sabah
State Minister of Education in December 2025.
7. Conclusion
Rural Sabah's combination of disconnected schools,
high teacher turnover, and the imminent shutdown of
SPLKPM creates a situation in which a digital CPD
solution is both necessary and timely. This report set
out to identify the requirements for such a solution,
grounded in the conditions that teachers, school
leaders, and district officers actually face. Starting
from BPG's strategic goal and working through three
ContextSquaREs and three personas, the
requirements in Section 6 address three connected
problems: teachers at disconnected schools cannot
access or complete CPD in any meaningful way, peer
learning and mentoring at school level is happening
but leaves no record and disappears when teachers
transfer, and district and national offices are losing
their only tracking system just as new policy targets
come into force.
The must-have requirements reflect this directly. An
offline-first mobile client with downloadable
micro-modules, image-based evidence submission,
and a queue-and-sync mechanism gives Liana a way
to complete and document CPD without relying on a
stable connection. A portable CPD record tied to
SGM 2.0 skill domains means her progress follows
her when she transfers, which matters in a context
Page 8

-- 8 of 10 --

where the average posting lasts two to three years.
The auditability requirement closes the gap left by the
shutdown of SPLKPM in 2026, without placing a
new compliance burden on teachers.
The should-have requirements extend this to Jainal
and Norhaida. A lightweight coordination layer for
peer learning and mentoring, a weekly school
summary pushed to WhatsApp, and a district
dashboard that aggregates SGM 2.0 data across
online and offline schools make the solution useful
beyond the individual teacher level. Integration with
DELIMa 2.0 and Program Duta Guru ensures the
solution fits into existing infrastructure rather than
duplicating it, which is important given the
investments already made in those programmes.
The could-have and won't-have categories reflect
honest constraints. A national BPG rollup mapped to
UNESCO ICT-CFT and full FS4A integration are
worth building eventually, but depend on partner
agreements and a published SGM 2.0 to ICT-CFT
mapping that does not yet exist. A pupil-facing client
and anything requiring continuous school
connectivity are out of scope for this release entirely,
in line with the conditions reported by the Sabah
State Minister of Education in December 2025 (The
Star, 2025).
Taken together, the requirements describe a solution
that is narrow enough to be buildable under real rural
constraints, and connected enough to the existing
policy landscape to be adopted and maintained after
the first release.
